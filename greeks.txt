Ancient Greek philosophy is one of the foundations of Western thought because Greek thinkers tried to explain the world, knowledge, ethics, and politics through reason rather than mythology. Its development began with the Presocratic philosophers, who focused mainly on nature, matter, and the structure of the cosmos. Earlier overviews of Ancient Greek philosophy consistently place figures such as Thales, Pythagoras, Heraclitus, Parmenides, Democritus, Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle among its central thinkers.

Among these figures, Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle are usually seen as the most influential. Socrates shifted philosophy toward moral questions and the examination of human life; Plato developed a powerful philosophical system centered on the theory of Forms; and Aristotle built a broad, systematic approach to logic, ethics, politics, and natural science. Standard summaries of the tradition describe this trio as the core of the classical period of Greek philosophy.

Other important philosophers also shaped the tradition in major ways. Heraclitus is associated with change and flux, Parmenides with unchanging being, Pythagoras with the philosophical role of number, and Democritus with early atomism. Together, these thinkers created many of the main questions that philosophy still asks today: What is reality, how do we know the truth, and what makes a good life?
